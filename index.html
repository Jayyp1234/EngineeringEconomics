Foundation · Week 1

What is Engineering Economics?

The core idea

Engineering Economics (ECN) is the discipline that helps engineers turn technical decisions into financially defensible ones. When you choose a material, design a refinery, or replace a pump, ECN tells you whether the choice makes economic sense — not just engineering sense.

Definition
Engineering Economics is the application of economic concepts, principles, and analytical tools to evaluate engineering options and guide decision-making.

Where ECN shows up — its scope

ECN isn't one task; it's the lens you use across many engineering activities:

  • Project evaluation — will this project yield economic benefits that justify the investment?
  • Comparing alternatives — picking the best among competing options.
  • Cost estimation — what will it actually cost?
  • Risk assessment — what could go wrong?
  • Profit (profitability) analysis — what returns will it bring?
  • Resource allocation & optimization — using scarce resources wisely.
Quick check
An engineer is deciding whether a new pump's energy savings justify its higher purchase price. Which scope area of ECN is being applied?

Foundation · The five costs

Basic Cost Concepts

Five cost types form the vocabulary of every ECN problem. Click any tile to see the lecturer's example.

F

Fixed Cost

Costs that remain constant regardless of output level — they don't change with how much you produce.
e.g. Salary of permanent staff
V

Variable Cost

Costs that vary with production or activity level. Produce more → spend more. Produce less → spend less.
e.g. Raw materials when building more bridges
S

Sunk Cost

Already committed and cannot be recovered. Should not affect future decisions — the money is gone whether you proceed or not.
e.g. Government refinery permit fee already paid
O

Opportunity Cost

The value of the next best alternative you forgo. Just a concept — you can't put a single figure on it. Forces engineers to consider trade-offs because resources are scarce.
e.g. Money locked in Project A can't fund Project B
I

Incremental Cost

The additional cost associated with a specific action. Often involves expansion or upgrades.
e.g. Cost of expanding production from 10 to 11 units

Variable cost vs. Incremental cost — a subtle distinction

These two get confused often. The lecturer was specific:

  • Variable cost — moves both ways with production (up or down). Tied only to production volume.
  • Incremental cost — only deals with the increase. It includes relevant variable costs plus sometimes new fixed costs (e.g. new machinery) that come from a specific business decision.

Maintaining a personal car isn't a variable cost — variable cost is associated with production only.

Role of Engineers in Decision-Making

Engineers are decision-makers.

The lecturer listed five concrete roles where engineers apply ECN tools:

1. Feasibility studies

Conduct technical and economic feasibility studies to evaluate alternative designs and investment options.

2. Material / process selection

Select materials and manufacturing processes that minimise both cost and environmental impact — because some activities have adverse environmental consequences.

3. Life-cycle cost optimization

Design for the whole life of the asset, not just the day it's bought. Consider:

  • Initial cost
  • Maintenance cost
  • Operational cost
  • Disposal cost

4. Investment advice

Advise management on capital investment decisions — should we acquire new equipment? Upgrade existing equipment?

5. Replacement decisions

Analyse maintenance costs and repairs for ageing infrastructure, and put replacement strategies in place.

Foundation · Week 2

Time Value of Money (TVM)

The single most important principle

The TVM principle
A naira today is worth more than a naira in the future.

Why? Three reasons the lecturer emphasised:

  • Interest — money today can be invested to yield returns.
  • Inflation — prices rise over time, eroding purchasing power.
  • Risk & uncertainty — you're certain of what you have today; the future is not.

"A bird in hand is worth ten in the bush."

Imagine these scenarios

A. Someone offers you ₦100k today.
B. Someone offers you ₦100k five years from now.

Scenario A is better — you can invest it, and future ₦100k will buy fewer goods anyway. The value of money depends on time.

Another scenario

A company offers two salary packages: ₦4m today, or ₦4.5m in 3 years. Don't rush. You can't decide until both cash flows are adjusted for time value at the prevailing interest rate.

The four fundamental TVM concepts

  • Present Value (PV) — the worth today of a future sum of money.
  • Future Value (FV) — the value a present sum will grow to when invested. ("If I invest ₦10m today, what will it grow to in 20 years?")
  • Discounting — the process of converting future cash flows back to present value.
  • Compounding — the process of projecting present money into the future.

Cash flows = a sequence of inflows (benefits) and outflows (costs) over time.

— Money walks forward via compounding and back via discounting —
viii.
Calculations

Simple & Compound Interest

Simple Interest (SI)

Applies only to the original principal. Interest does not earn its own interest.

Interest
I = P × i × n
Future Value
FV = P (1 + in)

where P = principal, i = interest rate per period, n = number of periods.

⚠ Rarely used in real ECN — it doesn't reflect actual investment behaviour. Bonds give higher returns than simple bank interest, so people prefer bonds over keeping money idle.

Compound Interest (CI)

Applies to both principal and accumulated interest. This is how money actually grows.

Future Value
FV = P (1 + i)n
Present Value
PV = FV / (1 + i)n

✓ Used in nearly all ECN analyses — it captures how money truly grows. Engineers prefer CI for loans, mortgages, and equipment leasing.

Worked example · Simple Interest
An engineer deposits ₦200,000 at 5% simple interest for 4 years. Calculate the simple interest and the amount received.
P = ₦200,000 i = 5% = 0.05 n = 4 years I = P × i × n = 200,000 × 0.05 × 4 = ₦40,000 F = P + I = 200,000 + 40,000 = ₦240,000
∴ Interest = ₦40,000 ; Total received = ₦240,000
Worked example · Compound Interest
If P = ₦500,000 at i = 0.08 (8%) compounded annually for n = 5 years, find F.
F = P (1 + i)ⁿ = 500,000 × (1 + 0.08)⁵ = 500,000 × 1.08⁵ = 500,000 × 1.46933 ≈ ₦734,650
∴ F ≈ ₦734,650

Why engineers prefer Compound Interest

  • It captures the real power of investment because it's compounded.
  • It is the basis used for loans, mortgages, and equipment leasing.
  • It captures the growth of savings and costs over time.

ix.
Concept

Economic Equivalence

Cash flows occurring at different times can be made equivalent if they have the same value once converted to a common time basis.

₦100k today may be equivalent to ₦140k in 4 years — depending on the interest rate.

The lecturer's parable

A man stole someone's cow ten years ago. After repenting, his clergy ask him to confess what he did wrong. He admits — but now that one cow has multiplied to 100 cows. The value of one cow then is equivalent to the value of 100 cows now.

2014 → 1 cow = ₦10,000 | 10 cows = ₦100,000
2024 → 1 cow = ₦100,000

The moral: equivalent value depends on time and rate. Engineers must always convert before comparing.

x.
Patterns

Cash Flow Patterns & Annuities

Engineers encounter three main cash flow patterns. Each has its own formula family.

1. Single cash flow

One-time cost or one-time benefit. Examples:

  • Buying a generator today (single cost)
  • Receiving compensation at the end of a project (single benefit)

2. Uniform Series (Annuity)

Equal payments at equal time intervals.

Used for: mortgage / loan repayments, installments, annual maintenance fees, lease payments, monthly subscriptions.

Annual amount given P
A = P · i (1+i)n / [ (1+i)n − 1 ]

Engineers use annuity to compare projects with annual costs.

3. Gradient Series

Cash flows that increase by a constant amount each period. Used for rising maintenance costs, predictable utility increases, escalating labour costs.

e.g. Yr 1: ₦10k Yr 2: ₦20k Yr 3: ₦30k → gradient = ₦10k

Worked example · Annuity
A loan of ₦1,000,000 at 10% interest is to be repaid over 5 years in equal annual installments. Find the annual amount A.
A = P · i (1+i)ⁿ / [(1+i)ⁿ − 1] = 1,000,000 × 0.10 × (1.10)⁵ / [ (1.10)⁵ − 1 ] = 1,000,000 × 0.10 × 1.61051 / 0.61051 = 1,000,000 × 0.26380 ≈ ₦263,797.48 per year
∴ Annual repayment ≈ ₦263,797.48 — total ≈ ₦1,318,987 over 5 years.

Engineering Economic Factors — the shortcuts

Rather than computing compound factors manually each time, engineers use named factors:

  • (P/F, i, n) — Present worth factor
  • (F/P, i, n) — Future worth factor
  • (A/P, i, n) — Capital recovery factor
  • (P/A, i, n) — Present worth (annuity) factor
  • (A/G, i, n) — Uniform gradient factor

These factors eliminate manual computation of compound factors every time.

xi.
Adjustment

Inflation & Real Interest Rate

Inflation reduces the purchasing power of money. Engineers must adjust the nominal interest rate to find what their real return is.

Fisher equation
1 + inominal = (1 + ireal)(1 + F)

where inominal = quoted bank rate; ireal = inflation-adjusted rate; F = inflation rate.

Worked example · Real Interest Rate
The nominal interest rate is 12%, inflation is 4%. Calculate the real interest rate.
1 + i_real = (1 + i_nom) / (1 + F) = (1 + 0.12) / (1 + 0.04) = 1.12 / 1.04 = 1.0769 i_real = 1.0769 − 1 = 0.0769 ≈ 7.69%
∴ Real interest rate ≈ 7.69% — what your money is actually earning.

Why does the real interest rate matter to engineers?

  • Comparing equipment costs across different years.
  • Evaluating salaries over time.
  • Projecting fuel costs.
  • Assessing maintenance cost growth.
  • Projecting future costs for any long-term project.

Nominal interest rate doesn't factor in inflation. The real interest rate is the one that matters.

xii.
Asset accounting

Depreciation in ECN

What is depreciation?

Depreciation is the loss of value of physical assets. It is caused by:

  • Wear and tear — physical degradation over use.
  • Technological obsolescence — using old, outdated equipment.
  • Usage intensity — using machines 24/7 without rest.

Why engineers care about depreciation

  • It affects taxes. Depreciation reduces taxable income — if you're meant to pay ₦10m in tax, depreciation reduces this.
  • It impacts replacement decisions. Helps you know when to change equipment.
  • It estimates actual operating cost. If you forget depreciation when computing operating costs, you can be dangerously misled — depreciation is part of operating cost.

Three depreciation methods

1. Straight Line (SL)

Constant amount of depreciation each year. Used when value is lost evenly.

Annual depreciation
D = (P − S) / n

P = purchase cost; S = salvage value; n = useful life.

2. Declining Balance (DB)

Used when an asset loses most of its value early. Depreciation is higher in the early years.

Year-t depreciation
Dt = Rate × Book value

3. Sum of Year Digits (SYD)

Assigns more depreciation in early years, less later. Useful when an asset is most productive (and most worn) early on.

Year-t depreciation
Dt = (P − S) × ( n − t + 1 ) / [ n(n+1) / 2 ]

where t is the current year (1, 2, … n).

Worked example · Straight Line
A machine is purchased for ₦2,000,000 with a salvage value of ₦200,000 and a useful life of 5 years. Find the annual depreciation D.
D = (P − S) / n = (2,000,000 − 200,000) / 5 = 1,800,000 / 5 = ₦360,000 per year
∴ The machine loses ₦360,000 in value every year.

Salvage value, in plain terms

S = the value left after you've deducted all depreciation. What you have left is your salvage value.

— SL is flat; DB and SYD front-load depreciation, but in different shapes —

Reality bites

Risk & Uncertainty

Risk

Future outcome is unknown, but the probability of each possible outcome can be estimated.
Risk = unknown outcome + known probability

Examples

  • A machine may break down with 25% probability next year.
  • Steel price has 40% chance of rising, 60% chance of falling.
  • A generator has 10% chance of needing major repair each year.
  • A project has 30% probability of cost overrun.

We can call them risks because we know the probability — from historical data, failure records, or market data.

Uncertainty

Future outcome is unknown — and the probability of outcomes is also unknown.
Uncertainty = unknown outcome + unknown probability

Examples

  • A new technology is introduced for the first time — we don't know how often it will fail.
  • Government may suddenly introduce a new regulation without warning.
  • A new material is used with no past performance data.
  • A plant is built in a region with no historical weather records.
  • A startup using a completely new engineering process.

We cannot estimate the probability — no historical data, the situation is too new, or events are unpredictable.

Major categories of risk in ECN

Market Risk
Fluctuations in market prices of inputs, outputs, or materials
Causes Supply & demand shifts, FX fluctuations, commodity cycles, inflation shocks, supply chain disruption. Engineering examples Steel price hike makes construction more expensive; diesel/petroleum price changes affect generator/truck operating cost; copper price flux affects cable cost; oil-price crash hurts refinery revenue. Impact Changes project cost estimates; affects operating cost projections; can flip a good project into a bad one (and vice versa).
Technical Risk
Failure of technology, equipment, or engineering design
Causes Poor design, manufacturing defects, equipment ageing, inadequate maintenance, operating conditions different from design assumptions. Engineering examples Manufacturing machine breaks down frequently; new solar inverter underperforms; water-treatment plant sees unexpected corrosion; building foundation fails due to soil misjudgement. Impact Unpredictable repairs & downtime, lower output, shorter equipment lifespan, higher replacement cost.
Financial Risk
Changes in financial conditions affecting project cost or returns
Causes Interest rate variability, FX fluctuations, inflation shocks, loan repayment changes, credit market tightening. Engineering examples Borrowing cost jumps from 12%→18% affecting cash flow; a Nigerian company borrowing in dollars sees naira depreciation push up debt burden; inflation raises spare-parts cost. Impact Reduces project profitability; may make some projects unaffordable; affects loan repayment schedules; increases uncertainty in long-term projections.
Regulatory / Political Risk
Government, policy, or legal changes
Examples New environmental laws raise compliance cost; tariffs change suddenly; permits are revoked or delayed.
Environmental Risk
Climate, weather, ecology
Examples Floods damage construction sites; droughts shut down hydropower; oil spills create remediation cost.

Part 2 · The toolkit

The Six Engineering Economic Factors

Why these six?

Engineers convert money from one point in time to another all the time. The lecturer named six factors that handle 95 % of TVM problems you'll meet:

  • P/F — Present given Future
  • F/P — Future given Present
  • P/A — Present given Annual
  • A/P — Annual given Present (capital recovery)
  • F/A — Future given Annual
  • A/F — Annual given Future (sinking fund)

Memorise the formulas — they are the spine of every PW, AW, and BCR comparison you do later.

1. P/F — Present given Future

How much is a future amount worth today?

P/F factor
P = F · 1/(1+i)n
Worked example
Find today's value of ₦500,000 payable in 3 years at 10 %.
i = 0.10, n = 3 P/F = 1/(1.10)³ = 1/1.331 = 0.7513 P = 500,000 × 0.7513 ≈ ₦375,650
∴ ₦375,650 today grows to ₦500,000 in 3 years at 10 %.

2. F/P — Future given Present

How much will today's money grow to in the future?

F/P factor
F = P · (1+i)n
Worked example
₦500,000 invested at 8 % for 5 years grows to?
F/P = (1.08)⁵ = 1.4693 F = 500,000 × 1.4693 ≈ ₦734,650
∴ ₦734,650 — money grows by ~47 % over 5 years at 8 %.

3. P/A — Present given Annual

Convert a stream of equal annual payments into a single present value. Used for loans, leases, annual maintenance.

P/A factor
P = A · [(1+i)n − 1] / [i(1+i)n]
Worked example
Annual savings of ₦200,000 for 4 years at 10 % — present worth?
P/A = [(1.10)⁴ − 1] / [0.10·(1.10)⁴] = 0.4641 / 0.14641 = 3.1699 P = 200,000 × 3.1699 ≈ ₦633,980
∴ ₦633,980 today is equivalent to ₦200,000/year for 4 years at 10 %.

4. A/P — Annual given Present (capital recovery)

The inverse of P/A. Use it for loan amortisation, capital recovery, equivalent annual cost.

A/P factor
A = P · i(1+i)n / [(1+i)n − 1]
Worked example
A ₦1,000,000 loan over 5 years at 10 % — annual payment?
A/P = 0.10 · (1.10)⁵ / [(1.10)⁵ − 1] = 0.16105 / 0.61051 = 0.2638 A = 1,000,000 × 0.2638 ≈ ₦263,800
∴ ₦263,800 per year repays the loan exactly.

5. F/A — Future given Annual

Future value of equal yearly deposits. Used in savings plans, sinking funds, replacement reserves.

F/A factor
F = A · [(1+i)n − 1] / i
Worked example
Save ₦100,000 yearly at 10 % for 4 years — final balance?
F/A = [(1.10)⁴ − 1] / 0.10 = 0.4641 / 0.10 = 4.641 F = 100,000 × 4.641 ≈ ₦464,100
∴ ₦464,100 accumulated after 4 years.

6. A/F — Annual given Future (sinking fund)

How much to set aside each year to reach a target future amount? Inverse of F/A.

A/F factor
A = F · i / [(1+i)n − 1]
Worked example
Want ₦1,000,000 in 5 years at 10 % — yearly deposit?
A/F = 0.10 / [(1.10)⁵ − 1] = 0.10 / 0.61051 = 0.1638 A = 1,000,000 × 0.1638 ≈ ₦163,800
∴ ₦163,800/year for 5 years builds the ₦1m fund at 10 %.

P/F vs A/F — when does the difference matter?

P/F doesn't say whether the result is yearly or biannual — it just gives you the present value of a single future amount. A/F specifically tells you it's annual. Watch the question wording: "yearly deposit" implies A/F; "today's value" implies P/F.

Part 2 · Decision criteria

Comparing Alternatives

Why we need formal methods

You're rarely picking from one option. ECN provides systematic, comparable bases — Present Worth (PW), Annual Worth (AW), Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) — to choose the alternative that yields the greatest economic advantage over time.

Fundamental principles before comparing

Four conditions must hold for any economic comparison to be valid:

  • Mutually exclusive alternatives — they relate to the same project / decision (you can buy a Toyota OR a Honda, not both).
  • Same planning horizon — assume all alternatives last for the same useful life (e.g. 10 years).
  • Consistent interest rate — the same MARR is used for every alternative.
  • Relevant cash flows only — sunk costs are ignored; only future costs and benefits attached to each option are considered.

Types of alternatives

  • Independent — each project decision doesn't affect another. Accept/reject each independently against criteria.
  • Mutually exclusive — selecting one automatically rejects the others (Toyota vs Honda).
  • Contingent — feasibility depends on another project being chosen first (battery storage requires solar panels first).

Minimum Attractive Rate of Return (MARR)

The lowest rate the firm will accept on an investment, given:

  • Cost of capital (interest paid on borrowed funds)
  • Inflation expectations
  • Risk level of the project (higher risk → higher MARR)
  • Opportunity cost (the next best alternative's return)

Also called hurdle rate or cut-off rate. MARR serves as the benchmark against which performance is measured.

1. Present Worth (PW) Method

Convert all future cash flows of each alternative into their equivalent value at time 0, using a given interest rate.

PW formula
PW = Σt=0 to n CFt / (1+i)t

For uniform annual benefit/cost with salvage:

Uniform-series form
PW = −C₀ + A · (P/A, i, n) + S · (P/F, i, n)

where C₀ = initial cost · A = annual benefit (or negative for cost) · S = salvage · n = life · i = MARR.

Decision rule: the alternative with the highest PW is economically preferred. (For costs only, pick the least negative.)

Worked example · Choose the better machine
Two machines, both 4-year life, MARR 10 %.
Machine A: cost ₦3,000,000 · annual benefit ₦1,200,000
Machine B: cost ₦4,200,000 · annual benefit ₦1,700,000
P/A factor = (1.10)⁴ − 1 / [0.10·(1.10)⁴] = 3.1699 Machine A: PW = −3,000,000 + 1,200,000·(3.1699) = ₦803,800 Machine B: PW = −4,200,000 + 1,700,000·(3.1699) = ₦1,188,830
PW(B) > PW(A) → Machine B is economically preferred.

2. Annual Worth (AW) Method

Converts all cash flows into an equivalent uniform annual amount over the project's life. Easier to compare alternatives with different lives if you assume infinite repetition.

AW formula
AW = PW · (A/P, i, n) · or · AW = −C₀·(A/P, i, n) + A + S·(A/F, i, n)
Worked example · Same machines, AW
A/P (10 %, 4) = 0.10·(1.10)⁴ / [(1.10)⁴ − 1] = 0.3155 Machine A: AW = −3,000,000·(0.3155) + 1,200,000 = ₦253,500 Machine B: AW = −4,200,000·(0.3155) + 1,700,000 = ₦374,900
AW(B) > AW(A) → Machine B preferred (same answer as PW, as expected).

3. Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR)

The ratio of present-value of benefits to present-value of costs. Most common in public-sector project evaluation.

BCR formula
BCR = PV(Benefits) / PV(Costs)

Decision rule:

  • BCR ≥ 1 → accept the project (benefits outweigh costs)
  • BCR < 1 → reject (cost exceeds benefit)
  • BCR = 1 → break-even, not making a profit or a loss
  • For mutually-exclusive public projects, choose the one with the highest BCR.
Worked example · Government road
Government considers a road project with PV(benefits) = ₦18 billion and PV(costs) = ₦12 billion.
BCR = 18,000,000,000 / 12,000,000,000 = 1.5
BCR = 1.5 ≥ 1, so accept. Each ₦1 invested yields ₦1.50 in benefits.

PW vs AW vs BCR — which to use?

  • PW: when alternatives have same horizon and you want the bottom-line lump-sum return.
  • AW: when alternatives have different lives (assume repetition) or when stakeholders think in annual budgets.
  • BCR: public-sector projects where benefit-to-cost transparency is required.
Quick check
Two mutually exclusive public projects — A: BCR 1.50; B: BCR 1.43. Which to pick?

Part 2 · Asset accounting

Depreciation Methods (deep dive)

Why this matters in ECN

Long-term assets (machinery, equipment, vehicles, infrastructure) lose value gradually. Depreciation is the systematic allocation of an asset's cost over its useful life. ECN uses it for:

  • Cost-based decision-making — accurate per-period cost
  • Determining taxable income — depreciation reduces taxable profit (real cash effect)
  • Estimating true project profitability
  • Replacement analysis — when to retire ageing equipment
  • Asset valuation and reporting

Depreciation is an accounting allocation, not a cash outflow — but its tax shield is real money.

Key terminologies

  • First Cost (FC) — initial purchase + installation cost
  • Salvage Value (SV) — expected value at end of useful life
  • Book Value (BV) — value recorded in accounts at a given time
  • Depreciable Amount = FC − SV
  • Useful Life (n) — expected service period (years/units)
  • Depreciation Charge (D) — annual allocated expense

Defender vs Challenger

In replacement analysis:

  • Defender = the asset you currently own
  • Challenger = the new asset you're considering buying

Compare PW (or AW) of keeping defender vs replacing with challenger. The asset with the higher present value wins.

1. Straight-Line (SL)

Equal value lost each year. Time-based. Most common in practice.

SL formula
D = (FC − SV) / n
Example · 5-year asset
FC = ₦12m, SV = ₦2m after 5 years. SL annual D?
D = (12,000,000 − 2,000,000) / 5 = 10,000,000 / 5 = ₦2,000,000 per year Year 0: BV = ₦12m Year 1: AD = ₦2m, BV = ₦10m Year 2: AD = ₦4m, BV = ₦8m Year 3: AD = ₦6m, BV = ₦6m Year 4: AD = ₦8m, BV = ₦4m Year 5: AD = ₦10m, BV = ₦2m (= salvage)
Straight-line: same ₦2m off the book each year.

2. Declining Balance / DDB

Accelerated — depreciation is higher early, lower later. Suits assets that lose value fast (technology).

DDB formula
Dt = k · BVt-1 ·· k = 2/n (DDB), 1/n (DB)
Example · 5-yr DDB
FC = ₦8.5m, SV = ₦500k, n = 5. Compute DDB schedule.
k = 2/5 = 0.4 (40 %) Year 1: D₁ = 0.4 · 8,500,000 = ₦3,400,000 → BV₁ = ₦5,100,000 Year 2: D₂ = 0.4 · 5,100,000 = ₦2,040,000 → BV₂ = ₦3,060,000 Year 3: D₃ = 0.4 · 3,060,000 = ₦1,224,000 → BV₃ = ₦1,836,000 Year 4: D₄ = 0.4 · 1,836,000 = ₦734,400 → BV₄ = ₦1,101,600 Year 5: D₅ = 0.4 · 1,101,600 = ₦440,640 → BV₅ = ₦660,960 BV₅ > SV (₦500k) — no need to floor it.
DDB front-loads depreciation. Watch BV doesn't go below SV in late years.

3. Sum-of-Years-Digits (SYD)

Another accelerated method, but smoother decline than DDB.

SYD formula
Dt = (RF/SYD) · (FC − SV) ·· SYD = n(n+1)/2 · RF = remaining life
Example · 4-yr SYD
FC = ₦6m, SV = ₦600k, n = 4. Yearly D?
SYD = 4·5/2 = 10 Depreciable = 6,000,000 − 600,000 = ₦5,400,000 Year 1 (RF = 4): D₁ = 4/10 · 5,400,000 = ₦2,160,000 Year 2 (RF = 3): D₂ = 3/10 · 5,400,000 = ₦1,620,000 Year 3 (RF = 2): D₃ = 2/10 · 5,400,000 = ₦1,080,000 Year 4 (RF = 1): D₄ = 1/10 · 5,400,000 = ₦540,000 Total = ₦5,400,000 ✓ (matches depreciable amount)
SYD weighting: 4/10, 3/10, 2/10, 1/10. Front-loaded but smoother than DDB.

4. Units of Production (UoP)

Usage-based, not time-based. Depreciation tracks actual production, not the calendar.

UoP formula
Dt = [(FC − SV) / total expected units] × actual unitst
Example · production-driven
Machine FC ₦15m, SV ₦3m, expected total output 120,000 units. Year 1 production = 20,000; Year 2 = 30,000; Year 3 = 25,000. Compute D each year and BV after Year 3.
D per unit = (15m − 3m) / 120,000 = ₦100/unit Year 1: D₁ = 100 · 20,000 = ₦2,000,000 → BV₁ = ₦13,000,000 Year 2: D₂ = 100 · 30,000 = ₦3,000,000 → BV₂ = ₦10,000,000 Year 3: D₃ = 100 · 25,000 = ₦2,500,000 → BV₃ = ₦7,500,000
UoP suits assets where wear depends on use, not age (e.g. mining equipment).

Part 3 · Mechanics & tax effects

Inflation, the Fisher Equation & Bracket Creep

Inflation in one paragraph

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over time, resulting in a decline in the purchasing power of money. The increase must be sustained — one-off price spikes don't count.

"What matters is not our money, but our purchasing power."

Indices that measure inflation

  • CPI — Consumer Price Index (tracks consumer basket prices)
  • GDP deflator — Gross Domestic Product deflator
  • PPI — Producer Price Index
Inflation rate
πt = (Pt − Pt-1) / Pt-1 × 100 %

Types of inflation

  • Demand-pull — excess demand over supply
  • Cost-push — rising production costs push prices up
  • Built-in — wage-price spiral
  • Speed-graded: Creeping → Walking → Galloping → Hyper

Hyperinflation is critical and severe — the worst kind.

Nominal vs Real Value — the money illusion

Nominal values are unadjusted current prices. Real values are corrected for inflation.

Real value
RV = NV / PI
(Real Value = Nominal Value ÷ Price Index)
Money illusion in action
Your salary rises from ₦950,000 to ₦1,000,000 — a 5 % rise. Prices rose 10 % in the same period.
Real raise = nominal raise − inflation ≈ 5 % − 10 % = −5 % Your purchasing power FELL even though the number on your paycheck went up.
This is money illusion — celebrating a nominal raise while your real welfare drops.

Fisher Equation — nominal vs real interest

Fisher equation (exact)
(1 + in) = (1 + ir)(1 + π)
Linear approximation
ir ≈ in − π

where in = nominal rate, ir = real rate, π = inflation.

Example I — find real rate
Nominal in = 15 %, π = 10 %.
i_r = i_n − π = 15 % − 10 % = 5 %
Real rate ≈ 5 %.
Example II — find real raise
Nominal income rises 12 %, inflation 10 %.
i_r = 12 % − 10 % = 2 %
Real income rises only 2 %.

Bracket Creep

Tax brackets are usually fixed in nominal terms. When inflation pushes your nominal income across a threshold, you pay more tax even though your real income hasn't risen.

Three things happen under bracket creep:

  • Higher tax bracket
  • Pay higher taxes
  • Lower real disposable income
Worked example · Bracket creep
Tax bracket: ≤ ₦1m taxed lightly (2 %); above ₦1m taxed heavily (10 %). Last year: salary ₦950,000 (under threshold). Inflation = 10 %, so to keep purchasing power, salary rises to ₦1,045,000.
Before: Gross ₦950,000 → tax 2 % = ₦19,000 Disposable = ₦931,000 After (no real raise, just inflation adjustment): Gross ₦1,045,000 → bumped above threshold! Tax 10 % = ₦104,500 Inflation deduction = ₦95,000 Disposable = ₦845,500 You earn the same in real terms, but pay ₦85,500 more in tax!
Bracket creep transfers purchasing power from you to government, silently, via inflation.

Indexation — neutralising bracket creep

Indexation is a policy that adjusts tax brackets periodically with the price index (typically CPI), so taxpayers aren't pushed into higher brackets purely by inflation.

Without indexation, three things happen:

  • Tax brackets are fixed nominally
  • Inflation pushes nominal wages up
  • Government revenue rises automatically — bracket creep

FIFO vs LIFO during inflation

Inventory valuation methods affect taxable profit during inflation:

  • FIFO (First In, First Out) — sells oldest, cheapest inventory first. During inflation, this inflates reported profits and raises tax bill.
  • LIFO (Last In, First Out) — sells newest, more expensive inventory first. Suppresses inflated profits.

Firms generally prefer LIFO during inflation because it lowers tax — but FIFO is more common in practice (and is required in some jurisdictions).

FIFO example
Bought 100 units in Jan @ ₦10, 100 in Mar @ ₦12, 100 in May @ ₦14. Sell 150 units. Selling price ₦20/unit. Compute FIFO cost & profit.
Cost (FIFO): 100 units @ ₦10 = ₦1,000 50 units @ ₦12 = ₦600 Total cost = ₦1,600 Revenue: 150 × ₦20 = ₦3,000 Reported profit = 3,000 − 1,600 = ₦1,400 But to replace inventory at current prices: 150 × ₦14 = ₦2,100 Real profit = 3,000 − 2,100 = ₦900 The extra ₦500 is "inflation gain" / paper gain — taxed even though it's not real.
FIFO + inflation = hidden tax increase. The lecturer flagged this as a key corporate-tax distortion.

Part 3 · Forecasting cost

Cost Estimation Techniques

The systematic forecasting of project cost

Cost estimation is the systematic process of forecasting financial resources needed to complete a project, deliver a service, or produce a product. Without reliable cost estimates, every other ECN tool — PW, NPV, BCR, replacement analysis — becomes meaningless.

Cost over-runs typically result from:

  • Project abandonment
  • Inefficient resource allocation
  • Loss of stakeholder confidence

Classification of costs

  • Capital / Initial cost — land, machinery, installation, commissioning, design
  • Operating & Maintenance (O&M) — labour, energy, fuel, repairs, parts, insurance, admin
  • Fixed vs Variable — fixed don't change with output (rent, salary); variable do (raw materials, utilities)
  • Direct vs Indirect — direct costs are easily traceable (materials, direct labour); indirect are overhead (security, supervision)

Qualitative / Judgement-based techniques

1. Expert Judgement

Relies on the experience & intuition of senior engineers (typically ~20 years' experience).

  • Steps: consult an experienced engineer · use historical knowledge · adjust for context
  • Advantages: fast, inexpensive; useful when data is scarce
  • Limitations: highly subjective, prone to bias and optimism

2. Analogy / Comparative Estimation

Estimate based on a similar completed project, scaled by an adjustment factor.

Analogy formula
Costest = Costsimilar × Adjustment Factor
Example
A 5km road costs ₦1bn. How much for a 7km road?
Adjustment factor = 7/5 = 1.4 Cost_est = 1bn × 1.4 = ₦1.4bn
Simple, useful early in planning. Limited by similarity of projects.

Quantitative techniques

1. Parametric Cost Estimation

Statistical relationships between cost and key parameters (capacity, size, power).

Parametric formula
C = a · Xb

where C = estimated cost, X = the parameter, and a, b are constants from historical data.

Example · Plant cost vs capacity
Historical fit: C = 500 · X0.6. Estimate cost for capacity X = 16.
C = 500 · 16^0.6 = 500 · 5.278 ≈ ₦2,639 (units = millions) ≈ ₦2.64 billion
Suitable for preliminary design — more objective than judgement, less detailed than bottom-up.

2. Capacity Factoring / Power Sizing Model

When you know the cost of a similar project at one capacity and want to estimate at another:

Power sizing
C₂ = C₁ · (Q₂ / Q₁)n

where n is the cost capacity exponent, typically 0.5 to 0.8 (chemical plants, power stations).

Example · Power plant scaling
A 100 MW power plant cost ₦120bn. Estimate cost of a 200 MW plant using n = 0.6.
C₂ = 120bn · (200/100)^0.6 = 120bn · (2)^0.6 = 120bn · 1.516 ≈ ₦182bn
Capturing economies of scale: doubling capacity costs only ~52 % more, not 100 %.

3. Detailed (Bottom-Up) Cost Estimation

The most accurate technique. Break the project into components, estimate each cost, sum the total.

Suitable for final budgeting; high accuracy but time-consuming and requires detailed design.

Cost Indices & Inflation Adjustment

Historical cost data must be adjusted to current prices using cost indices.

Cost index method
Cnew = Cold · (Inew / Iold)
Example
A project cost ₦80m in 2018 (Index = 150). Estimate in 2024 (Index = 210).
C_new = 80m × (210 / 150) = 80m × 1.4 = ₦112m
Common indices: CPI, CCI (Construction Cost Index), PPI.

Learning Curve Theory (Experience Curve)

Cost per unit declines as cumulative production experience grows.

Learning curve
Y = a · Xb·· b < 0

Y = cost per unit, X = cumulative output, a = first-unit cost, b is a learning constant.

Example: if labour hours fall 20 % whenever output doubles, that's an 80 % learning curve.

Used heavily in: manufacturing, defence projects, aircraft production.

Common errors & best practices

Common errors

  • Ignoring inflation
  • Over-optimism bias
  • Poor data quality
  • Technological uncertainty
  • Scope creep

Best practices

  • Use multiple estimation methods
  • Update estimates as project evolves
  • Document assumptions clearly
  • Incorporate contingency allowances
  • Conduct sensitivity analysis

Decision Making Under Risk & Uncertainty

The big picture

Decision-making is the most basic managerial activity — every other function (planning, organising, leading, controlling) depends on it. This section gives you the formal toolkit: how to MEASURE risk, how to make decisions when you DON'T know the probabilities, and how to choose between competing strategies under uncertainty.

By the end of this section, you should be able to: compute expected value, variance, standard deviation, and CV; build a payoff matrix; apply Laplace, Wald (Maximin), Maximax, Hurwicz, and Savage (Minimax Regret) criteria; and recommend a strategy.

Types of decisions

  • Programmed — fairly structured or recurring. Standard procedures handle them.
  • Non-programmed — unstructured, novel, infrequent. Each requires fresh judgment.

Engineering economic decisions are usually non-programmed and high-stakes — exactly where formal methods shine.

Three decision-making conditions

  • Certainty — alternatives and outcomes are known with reasonable certainty. Rare in practice.
  • Risk — outcomes uncertain, but their probabilities are known or estimable.
  • Uncertainty — outcomes uncertain AND probabilities unknown. The hardest case.

Risk vs. Uncertainty — the formal distinction

DimensionRiskUncertainty
Multiple outcomes?YesYes
ProbabilitiesKnown or estimable (from historical data)Unknown, cannot be estimated
Classic exampleCoin flip; stock with historical returns; insurance claimsFirst-of-its-kind regulation; pandemic-era projections
ToolsExpected value, variance, CV, decision treesMaximin, Maximax, Hurwicz, Laplace, Savage

Within uncertainty, two further sub-cases:

  • Complete ignorance — no probability assumptions at all. Decision criteria like Wald, Hurwicz, Savage apply.
  • Partial ignorance — manager assigns SUBJECTIVE probabilities from intuition / experience. Decision-making under uncertainty becomes decision-making under risk.

Measuring Risk: Expected Value, Variance, Standard Deviation, CV

1. Expected Value (Mean) — what you "expect"

For discrete random outcomes
E(x) = μ = Σ xᵢ · pᵢ

where xᵢ is each possible outcome and pᵢ is its probability.

Lecturer's example · Silver Zephyr Ltd.
Recession probability: 40 %. If recession: profit = $100. If no recession: profit = $1,000. Find E(π).
E(π) = p₁·π₁ + p₂·π₂ = 0.4 × 100 + 0.6 × 1,000 = 40 + 600 = $640
Expected profit = $640 — the mean across all possible outcomes weighted by their probability.

2. Variance — measuring spread

The mean alone hides crucial information about how WIDE the outcome distribution is. Two wagers might have the same expected value but very different risk profiles. Variance fixes that.

Variance formula
σ² = Σ (xᵢ − μ)² · pᵢ
Two wagers · same E(x), different risk
Wager 1: 50% chance of +$10, 50% chance of −$10.
Wager 2: 50% chance of +$1,000, 50% chance of −$1,000.
Compare their variances.
Both have μ = 0. Wager 1: σ₁² = 0.5(10−0)² + 0.5(−10−0)² = 0.5(100) + 0.5(100) = 100 σ₁ = √100 = 10 Wager 2: σ₂² = 0.5(1000)² + 0.5(−1000)² = 1,000,000 σ₂ = √1,000,000 = 1,000
σ₂² > σ₁² — Wager 2 is dramatically riskier despite identical expected values. The mean lies; variance tells the truth.

3. Standard Deviation

Standard deviation
σ = √σ²

Same risk ranking as variance, but in the same units as the original outcomes — useful for interpretation.

4. Coefficient of Variation (CV) — for unequal scales

Variance and standard deviation FAIL when comparing two projects with different expected values. CV solves this — it's a dimensionless ratio you can compare.

CV formula
CV = σ / μ
Project A vs Project B
Project A: μ = $100,000, σ = $30,000.
Project B: μ = $150,000, σ = $40,000.
Which is the relatively riskier project?
CV_A = 30,000 / 100,000 = 0.30 CV_B = 40,000 / 150,000 = 0.267 CV_A > CV_B
Even though Project B has the LARGER standard deviation in absolute terms, it is the relatively LESS risky project. This is the kind of trap that catches students in exams — always normalise by the mean.

Manager attitudes toward risk

How a manager values an uncertain payoff vs its expected value defines their risk attitude. Three archetypes:

Risk Loving
Convex utility — values payoff above expected value. Will gamble for upside.
Risk Neutral
Linear utility — values certain & uncertain payoffs equally if E[x] is the same.
Risk Averse
Concave utility — prefers certain payoff over uncertain at the same expected value.

Decision criteria under uncertainty (complete ignorance)

When you cannot assign probabilities, you need a rational rule to pick a strategy. The lecturer named four core ones plus their extreme cousin Maximax. They differ in how optimistic/pessimistic the decision-maker is.

Laplace Criterion (principle of insufficient reason)

"If we don't know any probabilities, assume they're all equal." Reduces uncertainty to risk.

Rule: Pick the strategy with the highest equally-weighted expected value.

Strength: simple, balanced. Weakness: equal probabilities are themselves an assumption that may not hold.

Wald (Maximin) Criterion — extreme pessimist

"Assume the WORST will happen for each strategy. Pick the strategy whose worst case is the LEAST bad."

Rule: max ( min row )

Best for risk-averse decision-makers protecting against catastrophe.

Maximax Criterion — extreme optimist

"Assume the BEST will happen for each strategy. Pick the strategy with the BIGGEST best case."

Rule: max ( max row )

Risk-loving — basically gambling. Rarely chosen by accountable managers.

Hurwicz Criterion — α-blended

A weighted average of best and worst case using a coefficient of optimism α (0–1).

Hurwicz value
H = α · (max row) + (1−α) · (min row)

α = 0 → Maximin · α = 1 → Maximax · α = 0.5 → balanced. Most realistic.

Savage (Minimax Regret) Criterion

Operates on opportunity cost, not raw payoff. For each state of nature, compute the regret of choosing the wrong strategy: |best payoff in that state − your strategy's payoff|. Then pick the strategy that minimises the maximum regret across all states.

"What's the worst regret I'll feel after the fact?" → minimise that.

Best for long-term capital projects where neither extreme optimism nor extreme pessimism is appropriate.

Break-Even Analysis & CVP

Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis — what and why

CVP analysis examines the interaction of a firm's sales volume, selling price, cost structure, and profitability. It's the most powerful short-term managerial tool you'll learn in this course.

Questions CVP answers:

  • How many units must we sell to break even?
  • How many units to earn a specific target profit?
  • Should we invest in automation (raising fixed cost, lowering variable)?
  • What's the impact of more advertising on profits?
  • How safe are we from a sales drop (margin of safety)?

Key CVP assumptions

  • Selling price is constant across the range
  • Costs are linear and split into variable and fixed
  • In multi-product firms, sales mix is constant
  • Production = sales (no inventory change)

These are simplifications. Real life violates them — but CVP still gives a defensible first-cut answer for short-term decisions.

Limitations

  • Linear cost & revenue functions — false at extremes
  • Fixed costs only "fixed" within the relevant range
  • Sales mix rarely truly constant in multi-product
  • Ignores estimation uncertainty in costs and prices
  • Production-and-sales-equal-each-period assumption is strong

The fundamental CVP equation

Profit equation
Profit = Sales − Variable expenses − Fixed expenses

Rearranged for analysis:

Solving form
Sales = Variable expenses + Fixed expenses + Profit

Contribution Margin (CM)

The amount each unit "contributes" toward covering fixed costs and then profit:

Per-unit and total
Unit CM = Selling price − Unit variable cost
Total CM = Sales − Total variable cost

Contribution Margin Ratio (also called C/S ratio or P/V ratio)

CM ratio
CM ratio = (Sales − VC) / Sales = Total CM / Sales

Break-Even Point (BEP) — two equivalent formulas

Break-even in UNITS
BEP units = Fixed expenses / Unit CM
Break-even in REVENUE
BEP sales = Fixed expenses / CM ratio

BEP is the level of sales at which profit = 0: total revenue exactly equals total cost.

Target profit — extending BEP

If you want a specific profit (not just to break even), add the target profit to fixed expenses:

Units to attain target
Units = (Fixed + Target profit) / Unit CM
Sales to attain target
Sales = (Fixed + Target profit) / CM ratio

Notice that BEP is just target-profit analysis with target = 0.

Margin of Safety (MoS) — your cushion before losses begin

The excess of budgeted (or actual) sales over the break-even sales. It tells you how much sales can drop before losses start.

In money
MoS (Rs) = Total sales − Break-even sales
As percentage
MoS % = (MoS / Total sales) × 100 %

Operating Leverage — magnifying profit changes

How much net operating income changes for a given % change in sales:

Degree of operating leverage
DOL = Contribution margin / Net operating income

If DOL = 5 and sales rise 10 %, profits rise 50 %. Higher DOL = more risk and more reward.

DOL is infinite at the break-even point (because operating income = 0). It falls as sales grow farther from BEP.

Worked Example 1 · Single product break-even

A new product: SP = Rs.60/unit · VC = Rs.40/unit · FC = Rs.25,000/month · Forecast = 1,800 units/month. Compute BEP units, BEP revenue, MoS %, and current profit.

Multi-product break-even — the constant sales-mix trick

Real firms sell many products. The key simplifying assumption: sales mix is constant — for every X units of A sold, Y units of B and Z units of C are also sold. Treat the whole portfolio as one "basket" or "mix".

Step-by-step:

  1. Compute unit CM for each product
  2. Compute contribution per mix = Σ (unit CM × units in mix)
  3. BEP in mixes = Fixed cost / Contribution per mix
  4. Convert: BEP units of each product = BEP mixes × ratio
  5. BEP revenue = sum of (BEP units × selling price)

Alternative — the average C/S ratio approach:

Average CM ratio (multi-product)
Avg CM ratio = Total contribution / Total sales
BEP sales = Fixed / Avg CM ratio

Worked Example 2 · Multi-product (PL example)

Product M: SP Rs.7, VC Rs.2.94. Product N: SP Rs.15, VC Rs.4.50. Mix: 5M : 1N. Fixed = Rs.36,000.
Unit CM: M: 7 − 2.94 = Rs.4.06 N: 15 − 4.50 = Rs.10.50 Contribution per mix: = 4.06 × 5 + 10.50 × 1 = Rs.30.80 BEP mixes: = 36,000 / 30.80 = 1,169 mixes BEP units: M: 1,169 × 5 = 5,845 units N: 1,169 × 1 = 1,169 units BEP revenue: M: 5,845 × 7 = Rs.40,915 N: 1,169 × 15 = Rs.17,535 Total = Rs.58,450
PL needs Rs.58,450 in sales (in the 5:1 mix) to break even.

Worked Example 3 · Target profit (multi-product)

Products F:G:H sold in ratio 2:1:3. SP = 22, 15, 19; VC = 16, 12, 13. Fixed = Rs.80,000. Target profit = Rs.52,000. Find the required sales of each product.
Unit CM: F=6, G=3, H=6 Contribution per mix = 6·2 + 3·1 + 6·3 = Rs.33 Required mixes = (80,000 + 52,000) / 33 = 4,000 mixes Required units: F: 4,000 × 2 = 8,000 → revenue 8,000 × 22 = Rs.176,000 G: 4,000 × 1 = 4,000 → revenue 4,000 × 15 = Rs.60,000 H: 4,000 × 3 = 12,000 → revenue 12,000 × 19 = Rs.228,000 Total revenue = Rs.464,000
Total Rs.464,000 in sales generates Rs.52,000 profit, given Fixed = Rs.80,000 and the 2:1:3 mix.

The Break-Even Chart — what's drawn vs what it means

The classic CVP graph (above, interactive) has:

  • Horizontal axis — units sold
  • Vertical axis — revenue / cost (Rs.)
  • Fixed-cost line — flat horizontal at FC value
  • Total-cost line — starts at FC, slope = unit VC
  • Total-revenue line — through origin, slope = SP
  • BEP — where TR meets TC (vertical difference = profit/loss)
  • Loss region — left of BEP (TR < TC)
  • Profit region — right of BEP (TR > TC), grows wider as volume rises

P/V Chart — the complement

A P/V (profit-volume) chart plots profit against sales volume directly. Easier for comparing alternatives at a glance.

  • At zero sales, profit = −Fixed cost (loss equals FC).
  • Profit line crosses zero at the break-even volume.
  • Slope of the line = Unit CM.

In a multi-product P/V chart, products are plotted in order of their C/S ratio — most profitable first. The dotted cumulative line shows how each product contributes; the solid average line shows the blended performance.

Sales mix and commissions — a cautionary tale

Salespeople pushed hardest based on commission, NOT contribution margin. The lecturer's example:

Simple Interest
I = P · i · n
P = principal, i = rate per period, n = periods.
SI Future Value
FV = P (1 + in)
Or simply FV = P + I.
Compound Interest FV
FV = P (1 + i)ⁿ
Used in nearly all ECN analyses.
Compound Interest PV
PV = FV / (1 + i)ⁿ
Discounting future to present.
Annuity (Capital Recovery)
A = P i (1+i)ⁿ / [(1+i)ⁿ − 1]
Equal annual payment from a present sum.
Real Interest Rate
1 + i_nom = (1 + i_real)(1 + F)
F = inflation rate.
Straight-Line Depreciation
D = (P − S) / n
Equal depreciation each year.
Declining Balance
D_t = Rate × Book value
Front-loaded; used when value drops fast.
Sum of Year Digits
D_t = (P−S)·(n−t+1) / [n(n+1)/2]
More depreciation in early years.
